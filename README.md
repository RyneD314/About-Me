# 👋 About Me – Ryne Dingler

I'm a Ph.D. candidate in Astronomy at Texas A&M University, expected to graduate in July 2026. I study the fastest, most extreme engines in the universe: **blazars** – supermassive black holes with jets pointed almost directly at Earth. Using high‑cadence optical data from NASA’s TESS satellite, I’ve characterized how these objects flicker on timescales from minutes to weeks, linked their variability to jet physics, and developed reproducible pipelines for power spectral analysis and light‑curve simulation.

But my work doesn’t stop at the telescope. I’m a mentor, educator, and advocate. I believe science survives only when scientists raise their voices – in the classroom, in the community, and in policy.

---

## 🔭 Research Snapshot

- **Paper I (Dingler & Smith 2024, ApJ)** – First large‑scale study of optical variability in southern TESS blazars. Found power‑law slopes of α ~ 2 and characteristic timescales of 0.8–8 days, consistent with jet‑dominated emission.
- **Paper II (in prep)** – A critical re‑examination of the rms‑flux relation using simulated light curves. Shows that optical rms‑flux correlations may be more complex than the simple linear relation seen in X‑rays.
- **IFU spectroscopy (VIRUS‑P / McDonald Obs.)** – Observing low‑redshift blazar host galaxies to separate jet contamination from stellar light, enabling black hole mass estimates via the M‑σ* relation.
- **Multi‑wavelength & mentoring** – Currently advising an undergraduate on a reverberation‑mapping project using TESS (optical), Swift (X‑ray), and Fermi (γ‑ray).

I’m proficient in Python, pipeline development (e.g., `quaver`), time‑series analysis, and integral‑field spectroscopy reduction.

## 🧑‍🏫 Teaching & Mentorship

Teaching is not a side job for me – it’s central to why I do science.

- **DEEP Program mentor (Texas A&M)** – For three years I’ve led teams of undergraduates in building year‑long physics & astronomy demonstrations for the annual Physics Festival (thousands of attendees). Projects include: a hydrogen‑line radio telescope, Python gravitational lensing simulations, electronics‑controlled rovers, and a 3D constellation model.
- **Physics Shows (Dr. Tatiana Erukhimova)** – I’ve helped present explosive, curiosity‑sparking demos to audiences from kindergarteners to grandparents.
- **Teaching Assistant (SMU & TAMU)** – Lab instruction and grading for introductory physics.
- **My philosophy** – Physics should be experienced, not feared. I use inquiry‑based learning, the DIANA framework, and tiered assessments to value effort and improvement over “right answers.” As a first‑generation college student and McNair Scholar, I’m committed to inclusive teaching that opens doors for students from all backgrounds.

## 📢 Advocacy & Policy

I’ve watched federal and state policies increasingly threaten science education, research funding, and the very positions that allow scientists to serve the public. That’s why I’m stepping beyond the lab.

- **John N. Bahcall Public Policy Fellowship applicant** – I want to help draft testimony, organize congressional visits, and fight for robust NASA/NSF budgets.
- **Grassroots action** – I’ve participated in protests against political interference in academic policy and contacted local lawmakers about education cuts.
- **Personal stakes** – I was supported by LSAMP and McNair – programs often targeted for cuts despite their proven return on investment. I owe my career to them, and I will defend them.

Even as I pursue postdoctoral and visiting professor positions (my ultimate goal is a college teaching career), I am also applying to civil‑service roles because **I refuse to stay silent** when science is under attack.
